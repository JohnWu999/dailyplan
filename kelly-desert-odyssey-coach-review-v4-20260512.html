Desert Odyssey
By: Kelly Zhang

1 Imagine this, walking in the desert, miles and miles away from civilisation. No Wi-Fi, no roads, only rock, dust, and sand. This is the Gobi desert. It is famous for its stars and sky, holding millions of bright stars. Believe it or not, we are those stars shining in the big vast sky. We may look small — but together, we can light up the entire sky.

2 In this trip of walking 68km, we are not walking by ourselves. Kids from all over China came to join for walking 68km, not only that, a six-year old kid came to walk too! We were a team. A good team needs a great captain and co-captain. The captain is kind, understands what a strong team looks like, and most importantly, has leadership. Without a captain, our team would not have finished the 68km. Our athletic co-captain showed true dedication by carrying three bags, despite the exhausting heat.

3 Another role that is important in the team is the flag raiser. He holds the flag up high to show teammates where the team is if someone falls behind. He is always at the front and helps to carry bags. He even warned the co-captain not to carry too many bags—but the co-captain did not listen, and as a result he grew exhausted and fell behind the team. Because we are in the desert, we need a GPS. I was the GPS, and my role is to guide the team. It may sound easy, but it is actually hard. Knowing which way to go climbing over which sand dunes… Sometimes, I make mistakes. I am not really satisfied by that because when I lead the group to the wrong direction, then we will have to find the right way and walk longer. When we were at big and tall hills, the group had to slow down to carefully cross these hills in case of anyone getting hurt. It was fun, being the GPS also making new friends! Our team was really successful.

4 Our biggest advantage is we encouraged the six-year old kid to finish the 68km. One time, there was a tall sand dune and the whole group had to cross over it, the wind was blowing ferociously making the team hard to go over that tall dune. The six-year old kid stopped at the middle of the way climbing, tears were streaking down to her chin, the wind blew ferociously at her, she was almost going to collapse! She thinks it is too hard for her to climb over this dune. Our teammates waited and encouraged her to not give up, we are about to finish this day! She brushed away her tears with her gloved hand and started to climb again. Our team was proud of her when she finished. Her father said that 50% of her credit is by herself and the other 50% is from the team that encouraged her to finish this 68km.

5 Beyond what we achieved together, the desert had its own private lessons waiting for me. In the desert, the day was hot and in the night was like ice. I not only needed to adapt to the weather, but also to overcome the challenge of walking for long hours. Three days of trudging through sand and scorching heat ground me down to my last drop of energy. Carrying a bag, plodding in the middle of nowhere, the sun as my only companion. In the night, the wind whooshed making the air biting into my skin, my hands were like ice. One day I woke up at 4:00 a.m, and outside it was like a refrigerator. I couldn't believe it. In this "Arctic", the team had to wake up and walk! I was sleepy and drowsy, when the cold air slapped me on the face I instantly woke up. After finishing this 68km I was happy and proud of myself. I learned that perseverance doesn't just carry you through 68km—on the other side, it reshapes who you are on the other side.

6 Looking back at the hills I went over, the roads I stumbled on, the challenges I overcame, I realise that the stars shining above us are not just there to shine. Every star, no matter big or small is there to remind you about the things you did to reach until here. Each grain of sand in the Gobi desert is like a small chapter of my life. All the struggles and happiness are in there, the made me who I am today. To end up, one big rock, is the new chapter that welcomes me.

01
Paragraph 1: Sharpen the Transition from "Imagine This" to the Stars
Structure

What I noticed: "Imagine this" is a great hook — but then the paragraph pivots from "walking in the desert" to "Gobi desert is famous for its stars" without warning. The reader is still in the physical desert; you want them to be in the starry sky. That pivot needs a bridge.

"This is the Gobi desert. It is famous for its stars and sky, holding millions of bright stars. Believe it or not, we are those stars shining in the big vast sky."

Your mission: After "This is the Gobi desert," add one sentence that physically connects the ground to the sky — something you notice at night when you look up. The stars are already there; just name the moment of looking. This bridges the opening scene to the star metaphor so it feels like one discovery, not two separate ideas.

Try This

Fill in the blank without overthinking:
"This is the Gobi desert. At night, _____ (one thing you see or feel when you look up)."

02
Two Remaining "Tell" Phrases — Replace with Body Sensations
Keywords

What I noticed: You have done strong sensory work in this draft — "the wind whooshed making the air biting into my skin," "my hands were like ice," the girl's tears. But two tell-phrases are still hiding:

"despite the exhausting heat" (Paragraph 2) and "the team was really successful" (Paragraph 3)

Your mission: Replace "exhausting heat" with what the heat did to one specific body part — the back of your neck, the soles of your feet, your water bottle. For "really successful," ask: what did we do that showed success? Describe the action, not the verdict.

Try This

Fill in the blanks:
"The _____ (body part) _____ (verb) from the heat."
"When we reached the top, _____ (what happened) — that was success."

03
Comma Splices: Three Places Where Sentences Run Together
Keywords

What I noticed: A comma splice is when you join two complete sentences with just a comma. Readers often don't notice these — but they notice the sentence rhythm feels off. You have three in this draft:

1. "tears were streaking down to her chin, the wind blew ferociously at her" — two complete thoughts
2. "encouraged her to not give up, we are about to finish this day" — two complete thoughts
3. "I was sleepy and drowsy, when the cold air slapped me on the face" — actually correct here (this one has "when")

Only the first two need fixing. Read aloud and pause where you breathe — that is where the period or semicolon should be.

Try This

Fix each splice by choosing a or b:
(a) Add a period and capitalise: "...chin. The wind..."
(b) Add a semicolon: "...chin; the wind..."
Do the same for the second splice. Read both versions aloud and pick the one that sounds right to your ear.

04
Closing: Fix the Fragments, Then Choose One Image
Idea

What I noticed: Paragraph 6 has the strongest raw material of the entire essay — and also its most frustrating errors. The rock image at the end ("one big rock, is the new chapter that welcomes me") is genuinely good, but it has three problems: a missing subject, a sentence fragment, and too many metaphors competing for space.

"All the struggles and happiness are in there, the made me who I am today." → missing subject ("they" or "these experiences")

"To end up, one big rock, is the new chapter that welcomes me." → the sentence structure needs fixing

Your mission: Step 1 — fix the missing subject: add "they" before "made." Step 2 — rewrite the final sentence about the rock using this structure: "One big rock — _____ — waits at the end of the 68km, a new chapter written in _____." The rock is your strongest closing image; make sure it is standing on solid grammatical ground.

Try This

Write two versions of the rock sentence:
Version A: describe what the rock looks like (colour, texture, size)
Version B: describe what it feels like to reach it (what does your body do when you stop?)
Pick whichever one makes you feel something.

The "Things You Did to Reach Until Here" — One Rewrite to Fix the Grammar
Keywords

What I noticed: This is one sentence with a grammar problem that has survived multiple drafts. It is worth fixing because the underlying idea is strong — the stars remind us of our journey.

"Every star, no matter big or small is there to remind you about the things you did to reach until here."

The phrase "the things you did to reach until here" has two issues: "to reach until here" is redundant (until here already implies reaching), and it is unclear what "to reach" is doing in the sentence.

Your mission: Try one of these three versions and pick whichever one preserves your meaning most clearly:

Try This

Version A: "Every star, no matter big or small, reminds us of how far we have come."
Version B: "Every star, no matter big or small, is there to remind you of what you endured to get here."
Version C: Write your own by starting with "Every star...reminds you of _____."
